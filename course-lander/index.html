Design is the first thing people see.

Whether we like it or not, first impressions matter. People do judge a book by its covers. You, or your clients, can have the most important content or product ever created, but it will be ignored if it doesn't come in the right package. Isn't that crazy?

That's why good design is important. It helps you communicate clearly to your target audience. It helps you stand out from all the noise and makes you look more trustworthy. It makes the most important information stand out.

As a marketer, you already know this. The barrier you face is knowing how to craft a good design - something that is both attractive and easy to understand.

Most marketers find themselves in one of two boats:

  1. Marketers who know design is important, but they don't think they can ever learn it. They mistakenly believe design is something you're born with - a skill you either have or you don't. They think back to their elementary school art class and cringe at how bad their work looked compared to everyone else's.
  2. Marketers who have taken steps to learn design on their own. They've read the blogs. They've used DIY tools like Canva or Piktochart. Yet, no matter what they do, they get stuck. Their designs are still missing that 'x factor' that other designs seem to have. They wonder how it is that some people magically seem to know how to put something together that looks amazing.

A common solution for both types marketers is to outsource their design-related tasks to a dedicated design team - whether in-house or on Upwork. But this can lead to a whole other set of problems.

How Most Marketers Approach Design

Consider your current process. Does it look something like this:

  1. Export a Word Document with all of the requirements and content for the design.
  2. Send the brief and task to an outside design team or in-house designer and impatiently wait for them to get to it.
  3. Get back an imperfect design that looks 'wrong' - either to you or the client
  4. Prepare and send feedback to the designer, even though you may not be able articulate exactly what is wrong.
  5. Rinse and repeat steps 3 and 4 until you get an acceptable design

If this sounds familiar, you realize just how limiting this process is. You can end up waiting hours or even days, when all you want to do is make a small change.

Besides that, you are still stuck with the task of giving feedback on the design and approving it. If you knew how to fix the design, you wouldn't even be using the design team in the first place - so why do they expect you to give good feedback.

The truth is: Design is a skill that can be learned.

It's a skill just like riding a bike. And as with any skill, you can struggle to learn it on your own - or you can hire a coach to teach you the secrets behind design and how to avoid the common pitfalls beginners face.

FAQ

Why PowerPoint? Will this course benefit me if I choose to use another tool?

A common misconception about design is that you need expensive software to be able to create something great. This couldn't be further from the truth.

I'm not here to teach you the minutia of Adobe Photoshop or Illustrator. I'm here to help you create designs quickly using a commonly available tool. The principles you will learn in this course will carry over to more sophisticated programs when you're ready for them.

You need to start somewhere. Filmmaker, Robert Rodriguez, didn't make his first movie with a dedicated film crew and multi-million dollar budget. He used what he had on hand. Stop looking for excuses or the 'perfect tool'.

A million dollar tool in the hand of an amateur is no match with a $100 tool in the hands of a professional.

An advanced tool will overwhelm a beginner. My philosophy is that you should only upgrade when you can't do something with your current tool. For 90% of marketers, PowerPoint is all you'll need: it has WYSIWYG capability, drag-and-drop functionality, and the ability to experiment effortlessly.

Will I be able to integrate what I learn with my company's current processes or workflows? For instance, can I generate a PSD or AI file.

Sadly, there is no way of doing this natively from PowerPoint. If you truly need to be able to generate these types of files, I would recommend not joining. The benefit to using PowerPoint is the easier learning curve and ability to make faster progress.

Are you saying I can create entire designs using only PowerPoint?

Yes and no. PowerPoint can be used to mockup webpages and apps, but not to create them.

However it can be used to create entire infographics and E-Books too. Some of the things are going to be done completely in PowerPoint - others like websites and apps are not.
